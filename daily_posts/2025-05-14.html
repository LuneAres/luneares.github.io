Unitary representations of the Heisenberg group: CCR to express it in the algebra

Okay, I have a few things I wanna talk about but I don’t have time to write everything down tonight. Let’s sum things up here.

The idea is that using Stone’s theorem on one-parameter unitary groups we can match unitary (irreducible) representations of the Heisenberg group with (irreducible) a class of representations of the algebra (hum, maybe this correspondance appear in the proof of Stone’s theorem).1

A representation of the heisenberg group is totally caracterized by the representation of the one-parameter subgroups generated by \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\).

If we consider \(\exp(xX) \in H\) an element of the one parameter subgroup generated by \(X\), and \(\rho: H \to \U(\H)\) an unitary representation, then we have (by Stone’s theorem) \(\rho(\exp(xX)) = e^{ixT_X}\) with \(T_X \in \L(\H)\) self-adjoint (and potentially unbounded, but densily defined2). And in a similar fashion we can define \(T_Y\) and \(T_Z\).

And if \(\rho\) is irreducible we have that \(e^{izT_Z} = \rho(\exp(zZ))\) is a multiplication operator, so \(T_Z = \lambda\Id_\H\) with \(\lambda \in \R\). And we have \(e^{ixT_X} e^{iyT_Y} = \rho(\exp(aX) \exp(bY)) = \rho \big( \exp(bY) \exp(aX) \exp(abZ) \big) = e^{ibT_Y} e^{iaT_X} e^{iab\lambda \Id_\H} = e^{iab\lambda} e^{ibT_Y} e^{iaT_X}\)

In the end what we get is a caracterization of the representations of the Heisenberg algebra corresponding to unitary irreducible representations of the Heisenberg group:

Choose \(\lambda \in \R\) and two self-adjoint operators \(p\), \(q\) verifying

\[\begin{align}\tag{$\ast$} e^{isp} e^{itq} = e^{ixy\lambda} e^{itq} e^{isp} \end{align}\] for any \(s,t \in \R\). The algebra representation \(\pi: \h \to \L(\H)\) is defined by \(\pi(X) = p\), \(\pi(Y) = q\), and \(\pi(Z) = \lambda \Id_\H\). We can check that it indeed descends to a unitary representation \(\rho: H \to \U(\H)\) defined by \(\rho(\exp(A)) = e^{i\pi(A)}\). (I started writing down the calculations but I’ll finish later)

We call the relation \((*)\) the “Weyl’s formulation of the canonical commutation relation” (CCR) for \(p\) and \(q\).

Another formulation of the CCR is Heisenberg’s (I think that’s the first we usually see, and that’s the first given historically): \[\begin{align}\tag{$**$} pq - qp = -i\hbar \Id_\H. \end{align}\]

I switched \(\lambda\) to \(\hbar \in \R\) (the reduced Planck constant) because this value corresponds to a physical observation of a small commutativity failing when we take \(p\) and \(q\) the position and momentum operators respectively.

The Heisenbergs formulation can be easily derived from the Weyl’s formulation by differentiating \((*)\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\) around \((0,0)\), but going from \((**)\) to \((*)\) is actually not possible

Actually, doing formal calculations we can also derive \((*)\) from \((**)\) starting with commuting relations between \(p\) and \(q^n\), then polynomials in \(q\), and extending to the series \(e^{itq}\) (since we know from Stone’s theorem that it is well defined), and then between \(p^n\) and \(e^{itq}\), then polynomials in \(p\), and finaly between \(e^{isp}\) and \(e^{itq}\). But the results of those calculations only hold on the domain of definition of the objects at play, so we can’t consider any pair of self-adjoint operators \((p,q)\) since the domain of definition of the product (=composition) of the two operators may even be \(\{0\}\).

In order to be able to use Heisenberg’s formulation, on must add some condition on \(p\) and \(q\), such as the one Kirillov refers to as “Edward Nelson Condition”.3
