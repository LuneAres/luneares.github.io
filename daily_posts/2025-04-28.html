Recap of what I talked about with Clément

Today I talked with Clément, and here is a list of things he told me (with varying level of explanation).

I’ll try formalize things later, here I dump basically everything I remember:

  • Definition of arbitrary vector bundle over a manifold \(M\): locally it looks like the trivial bundle.

    So you take an open covering \(\{U_i\}_i\) of \(M\) such that the bundle is supposed to look trivial on any \(U_i\), you consider the disjoint union \(\bigsqcup_i U_i \times \R^N\) of the trivial bundles and you glue everything together by identifying some points of \(U_i \times \R^N\) and \(U_j \times \R^N\) when \(U_i \cap U_j \neq \emptyset\), using a family of diffeormorphisms (seeing every \(U_i \times \R^N\) as a product of manifolds) \[\begin{array}[t]{rccc} \varphi_{ij}: & U_i \cap U_j \times \R^N & \to & U_j \cap U_i \times \R^N \\ & (x \in U_i, \xi) & \mapsto & (x \in U_j, f_{ij}(x, \xi)) \end{array}\] where for every \(x \in U_i \cap U_j\) the application \(f_{ij}(x, \,\cdot\,): \R^N \to \R^N\) is a vector space isomorphism.

    Additionaly, for the identification to work you need the family of diffeomorphisms \(\{\varphi_{ij}\}_{i,j}\) to verify the Čech cocycle property, so that you really get an equivalence relation \(\sim\), and the vector bundle is given as \(E = \big( \bigsqcup_i U_i \times \R^N \big) / \sim\)

    Every possible \(N\)-dimensional vector bundle is then defined by varying the family \(\{f_{ij}\}_{i,j}\) (and the covering)

    If you used a covering \(\{U_i\}_{i \in I}\) and a family \(\{f_{ij}\}_{i,j \in I}\) to define your vector bundle, then any subcovering \(\{U_i\}_{i \in J}\) for \(J \subset I\) and family \(\{f_{ij}\}_{i,j \in J}\) defines the same vector bundle.

    And the other way around: if the vector bundle is already defined using covering \(\{U_i\}_{i \in I}\), you can add any open set \(U_\alpha\) to you covering, and the diffeomorphisms \(f_{\alpha \alpha}, f_{\alpha i}, f_{i \alpha}\) for \(i \in I\) are already entirely defined by the cocycle property, and logically won’t change which vector bundle you’re looking at.

    I’d need to check but I feel like it’s no use trying to define a vector bundle using a covering of \(M\) finer than the covering given by an atlas of \(M\).

    What I mean by that is that if there is an atlas \(\A\) on \(M\) with a covering coarser than the one you used to define your vector bundle then you could have used the covering given by \(\A\) to define the same bundle)

  • When constructing the tangent bundle we basically just take the maps \(\{f_{ij}\}_{i,j}\) to be the tangent map of the change of charts of any atlas.
  • Only two possible line bundles over the circle: the trivial bundle and one that looks like a mobius strip (here are multiple proofs of this, the last answer seems accessible).

    If my assumption above is true, then you could start by trying prove that any open covering of the circle admits a subcovering that is either trivial, or finer than a covering given by two stereographic projections. And then you could categorize the possible line bundles in the second case (the trivial covering case allows only for the trivial bundle).

    I don’t know if either of the two steps work, or if my original assumption is true, but I thought about that because I asked myself “why couldn’t there be more twists to the mobius strip?”

  • Definition of the tensor product by the universal property.
  • Definition of the spaces of symmetric and antisymmetric tensors.
  • Definition of a volume form (a non-vanishing section of \(\bigwedge^n TM\)) to get a measure on the manifold from the Lebesgue measure on Rⁿ.
  • A vector field over a Lie group invariant under the action induced by the left (or right) multiplication is entirely determined by its value on the Lie algebra (ie. tangent space around the identity).
  • Apparently we can always find a vector field on a Lie group that is invariant under the action induced by left multiplication (as well as one for the right multiplication)
  • If we want a vector field that is invariant by both left and right actions than what we want is a vector field that is invariant by the action induced by the conjugation.
  • We can’t always find such a vector field, but for compact Lie groups we can because we can use a volume form invariant under the group multiplication (why is there one?) to construct a measure on the Lie group, which is finite if the group is compact, allowing us to take an average () and get a measure invariant by congugation.
  • A 1-form is a section of the cotangent bundle.
  • Saying “I’ll look at this real matrix as a complex matrix” is using \(\R^n \otimes \C\) as \(\C^n\).
  • Construction of a Lie group from a nilpotent Lie algebra using the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula for the exponential map.
  • If you know a matrix realisation of a nilpotent Lie algebra1 you can use the usual exponential map on matrices to realise the Lie group as a subgroup of \((\GL_n, \circ)\).

    And I feel like it also works if you replace \((\M_n(\R), +, \circ)\) by any Banach algebra2 \((\mathcal{A}, +, \,\cdot\,)\) such that you can realize the Lie algebra you’re interested in by using the commutator as the lie bracket: \([X,Y] = X \cdot Y - Y \cdot X\) for \(X,Y \in \mathcal{A}\).
