Well, let’s still give some mathematical fun facts!

A connected topological group is generated by any neighbourhood of the identity.

Proof.
You take \(U\) a neighbourhood of the identity and \(H\) the generated subgroup.
The set \(U\) gives you a neighbourhood of any element \(g \in G\), namely: \(gU \in \mathcal{V}(g)\).

And for any \(g \in H\), since \(H\) is a group and \(U \in H\), we have \(gU \subset H\).
So we have two things:

  • \(H\) is open.
  • \(H = \bigcup_{g \in H} gU\)

Now if we consider \(x\) in the boundary of \(H\) we have \(xU \cap H \neq \emptyset\).
Meaning there exists \(g \in U\) so that \(xg \in H\).
Meaning that \(x = (xg)g^{-1} \in H\).
So \(H\) contains its boundary: it’s closed.

Finally, if \(G\) is connected and \(H \neq \emptyset\) we then have \(H = G\). \(~~~\square\)

In fact we have this:

Every open subgroup of a topological group is also closed

Proof.
We do the same thing as in the previous proof, but since \(H\) is open and contains the identity, we take \(xH\) as a neighbourhood of \(x\) (we could have also done it that way in the previous proof).

If we consider \(x\) in the boundary of \(H\) we have \(xH \cap H \neq \emptyset\).
Meaning there exists \(g \in H\) so that \(xg \in H\).
Meaning that \(x = (xg)g^{-1} \in H\).
So \(H\) contains its boundary: it’s closed. \(~~~\square\)

It’s not true the other way around (we have “open \(\Rightarrow\) close” but not “closed \(\Rightarrow\) open”).

For exemple, in any non-discrete group the singleton \(\{e_G\}\) isn’t open, and it’s at least closed for groups topologically embedded in \(\R^n\), or for Lie groups since the singleton is then homeomorphic to a singleton of \(\R^n\).

This example doesn’t work for every non-discrete topologies though.
For example (yes, a counter-counter-example), we could put the trivial topology on any group \(G\) to make it a topological group (which make any permutation of \(G\) continuous : in particular, the left and right multiplication and the inversion).
However, for now I can’t give a concrete non-trivial example of a topological group that appears naturally for which the singletons aren’t closed (or even a topological group that isn’t discrete or Lie, or the product of some).

I corrected what was written after that: when I initially wrote this post I gave \(\Q < \R\) as an example of a “closed but not open” subgroup of \(\R\)…

Another example is \(\Z < \R\), which is also closed because it’s discrete, but not open.

And to give an example of non-discrete closed proper subgroup of \(\GL_n(\R)\) (for \(n \geq 2\)) I can think of \(\ker(\tr) \cap \GL_n(\R)\), which is closed in \(\GL_n(\R)\) because the kernel of the trace is closed in \(M_n(\R)\) since it’s a hyperplane of \(\M_n(\R) \cong \R^{n^2}\).
And if you know that \(\GL_n(\R)\) has two connected components, characterized by the sign of the determinant (I don’t know why it’s the case, though), than you can see that our subgroup is neither of them (it has elements in both, no matter \(n \geq 2\)), so it isn’t open.
Otherwise you can just see for example that any diagonal invertible matrix \(D\) with null trace is in the boundary our subgroup since it can be approached by invertible matrices \(D + \lambda I_n\) for \(\lambda > 0\) arbitrarily small (you just avoid any value that appears in the coefficients of \(D\)). So the subgroup cannot be open, since it contains an element of it’s boundary.
