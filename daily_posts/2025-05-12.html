First part of constructing an irreducible unitary representation from a linear form on the Lie algeabra

Friday was quite a busy day so I didn’t find time to write a post, but I’ve met Clément, and I also gave some thinking to a few things on the week-end, so I will talk about it (as much as I can before being too sleepy).

Answering the exercise from last wednesday

First, Clément gave me the reason of the correspondance between closed subgroups and Lie subalgebras :

  • if you have a closed subgroup of a Lie group \(G\), than by Cartan’s theorem it is an embedded Lie group (well, I still need to look for how this works), and it’s tangent space around the identity is a Lie algebra embedded in the lie algebra of \(G\): a Lie subalgebra
  • the other way around I already had it: if you take a Lie subalgebra of the Heisenberg algebra (or any nilpotent Lie algebra), the BCH formula basically gives you the product of \(\exp(A)\) and \(\exp(B)\) for \(A\) as the exponential of a linear combination of \(A\), \(B\), and their nested Lie brackets up to a certain order (since the algebra is nilpotent it stops at some point)

First part of constructing an irreducible unitary representation from a linear form on the Lie algeabra

I also saw how any linear form \(F\) over the Lie algebra of a nilpotent Lie group naturally induce an application \(\pi_F: (\exp(\h), \circ) \to (\S^1, \times)\) such that that following diagram commutes (since the exponential map is a bijection in the case of a nilpotent Lie group):

Namely, for any \(\exp(A) \in \exp(\h)\) we have \(\pi_F(\exp(A)) = e^{2i\pi F(A)}\).

In general the application \(\pi_F\) isn’t a group morphism.

For example, if you look at \(\h\) the Heisenberg algebra and take a \(F \in \h^*\) then you have the following \[\begin{align*} \pi_F(\exp(A)\exp(B)) &= \pi_F(\exp(A+B+{\textstyle\frac{1}{2}}[A,B])) \\ &= e^{2i\pi F(A+B+{\textstyle\frac{1}{2}}[A,B])} \\ &= e^{2i\pi F(A)} e^{2i\pi F(B)} e^{2i\pi {\textstyle\frac{1}{2}}F([A,B])} \\ &= \pi_F(\exp(A)) \pi_F(\exp(B)) e^{2i\pi {\textstyle\frac{1}{2}}F([A,B])} \end{align*}\] So we have \(\pi_F(\exp(A)) \pi_F(\exp(B)) = \pi_F(\exp(A)\exp(B))\) if and only if \(e^{2i\pi {\textstyle\frac{1}{2}}F([A,B])} = 1\).

Depending on \(F\), this may not be verifie for every couple \((A,B) \in \h^2\).
So if we want to restrict \(\pi_F\) to a group isomorphism we need to find a subgroup \(\exp(\mathfrak{a}) < \exp(\h)\), where \(\mathfrak{a} < \h\) is a Lie subalgebra (we only care about closed subgroups)

We have three options to constrain the values of \(A\) and \(B\) (from the more restrictive to the least restrictive):

  1. \(\forall A,B \in \mathfrak{a},\, [A,B]=0\)
    ie. we take \(\mathfrak{a}\) the center of the algebra: \(\mathfrak{a} = \R Z = \exp^{-1}(Z(H))\), where \(Z(H) = \exp(\R Z)\) is the center of the Heisenberg group
  2. \(\forall A,B \in \mathfrak{a},\, F([A,B])=0\)
  3. \(\forall A,B \in \mathfrak{a},\, \frac{1}{2}F([A,B]) \in \Z = \ker(x \mapsto e^{2i\pi x})\)

The last one actually falls back to the second one.
If the last constraint is verfied we have this partition of the set of allowed values for the bracket: \[[\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}] \subset F^{-1}(2\Z) = \bigsqcup_{n \in \N} F^{-1}(2n)\] where each \(F^{-1}(2n)\) is both open and closed (they may not be connected, but they’re unions of connected components).
On the other hand, the bracket being continous from \(\h \times \h\) to \(\h\) we have that \([\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}]\) is connected in \(\h\) because \(\mathfrak{a}\) is connected in \(\h\) (linear subspaces are path-connected), so \(\mathfrak{a} \times \mathfrak{a}\) is connected in \(\h \times \h\).
So we must have \([\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}] \subset F^{-1}(2n)\) for some \(n \in \Z\), and since we have \([0_\h,0_\h] = 0_\h \in F^{-1}(0)\) we must have \(n=0\), which is exactly the second constraint

the last condition gives us multiple diconnected components of \(\h\) as values allowed for the bracket (\([\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}] \subset F^{-1}(2\Z)\), and \(F\) is continuous, and \(2\Z\) is discrete so disconnected). So since \(\mathfrak{a} < \h\) is connected, and \(\mathfrak{a} = [\mathfrak{a},0] \subset [\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}]\), it is contained in the connected component containing 0: \(F^{-1}(\{0\})\).

Both (1) and (2) are pretty straightforward and still work for higher order nilpotent Lie abebra.

And the second one is pretty neat: it’s (possibly) more permissive than the first one (the permissiveness depends on \(F\), if I take \(F: xX+yY+zZ \mapsto z\) then the second condition implies the first one), but it is still really simple.
It’s the one we’ll use.

I don’t know exacly how it goes for nilpotent Lie groups other than Heisenberg but I feel like conditions less restrictive than the second one start getting messy when nested commutators get involved and you need to manage linear combinations of those.

Does it actually always fall back to the first or the second condition?

I still have to understand what is going on but the key thing apparently is that if you take \(\mathfrak{a}\) a subalgebra that is maximal for condition (2) then the “induction functor” will spit out an unitary irreducible representation of \(\exp(\h)\) from the character \(\pi_F|_{\exp(\mathfrak{a})}\) over \(\exp(\mathfrak{a})\)

Starting to think about infinite dimensional unitary representations

Something I realized when I tried to induce an algebra representation from an unitary representation of a Lie group is that if you consider an infinite dimensional hilbert space \(\H\) then the space of unitary operators on \(\H\) isn’t a Lie group. Otherwise I managed to find the reasoning to establishing that the unitary group of order \(n\) is a \(n^2\)-dimensional real manifold, the associated Lie algebra being the skew hermitian matrices.

I’m really sleepy now so I don’t go into details (I still have a lot of things to figure out), but apparently Stone’s theorem on one-parameter unitary groups allows you to still define the derivative of a unitary representation around the identity.
